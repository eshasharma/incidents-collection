Privacy






News about Privacy, including commentary and archival articles published in The New York Times.
 

Chronology of Coverage


Jan. 28, 2016
Nick Bilton Disruptions column examines growing controversy over drone use and outrage from neighbors of drone owners who feel spied upon; recounts his own experience of having his privacy invaded when drone with camera flew close to his window.	           MORE



Jan. 25, 2016
Isabelle Falque-Pierrotin, France's top privacy regulator, is at center of attempts to monitor how digital data is transferred out of European Union; her efforts mark latest standoff between Europe and American tech firms; Falque-Pierrotin's role could grow even further if American and European negotiators fail to achieve new data-transferring agreement by Feb 1. 	           MORE



Jan. 18, 2016
European Union antitrust chief Margrethe Vestager fires warning shot at companies like Google and Facebook, saying that their widespread collection of user data might run afoul of Continent's competition rules; Vestager's voice joins chorus of other European officials who are growing increasingly assertive about data protection and digital privacy. 	           MORE



Dec. 31, 2015
Database of personal information from 191 million voter records is posted online, raising privacy concerns; experts say most of information was available elsewhere, but consolidation in one place makes it attractive to identity thieves; it is unknown who built database, which is no longer publicly accessible.	           MORE



Dec. 30, 2015
Op-Ed article by author Rebecca Skloot anticipates sweeping changes to Federal Policy for Protection of Human Subjects, or Common Rule, governing use of human tissue and genetic material for research, and to related consent rules; holds use of tissue is likely to expand and benefit many in era of precision medicine, but carries significant privacy concerns; notes debate over extent of proposed consent practices and invites public response.	           MORE



Dec. 27, 2015
Sweden is moving away from use of cash, where many people pay for goods and services using mobile apps and credit cards; digital payments offer ease of use, and many of country's banks have stopped accepting or dispensing cash; critics of trend warn of increased threat to privacy and vulnerabilty to Internet crimes. 	           MORE



Dec. 27, 2015
Natasha Singer Technophoria column examines innovations at the Josie Robertson Surgery Center, new $300 million outpatient facility founded by Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center in New York City; notes facility's focus on patient data-gathering could lead to improved service, but also treads upon ethical grey area in which patients' rights are not firmly established by law. 	           MORE



Dec. 24, 2015
Loopholes in the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act are allowing health providers to disclose potentially damaging information about patients as they pursue debts; problem is exemplified by actions of Short Hills Associates in Clinical Psychology, New Jersey group that has filed dozens of lawsuits against patients that disclosed diagnoses and treatments.  	           MORE



Dec. 24, 2015
Many New Yorkers have resorted to wearing headphones without any sound while at work or during their commute in effort to avoid being bothered by others.	           MORE



Dec. 21, 2015
Editorial warns new online privacy bill being considered by European Union could infringe upon free speech; asserts measures such as requiring companies to explicitly request permission to use consumer's data, and allow its transfer to other services, are offset by expansive right of users to delete information about themselves from search results.	           MORE



Dec. 21, 2015
Advertising column; Digital Advertising Alliance's AdChoices has small turquoise triangle logo that offers consumers way to opt out of receiving interest-based ads; survey finds only 9 percent of web users understand logo's meaning.	           MORE



Dec. 16, 2015
European officials approve long-awaited data protection rules, which would give people more control over collection and management of their digital information; changes will take effect by early 2017.	           MORE



Dec. 1, 2015
Op-Ed article by Andrew Keane Woods observes that countries around world are increasingly exercising territorial control over Internet and Internet users' data, largely due to concerns about fact that much of Internet data is controlled by United States companies; underscores need for some kind of international agreement to govern Internet surveillance, and, in meantime, urges US companies to make it easier for foreign law enforcement agencies to access data.	           MORE



Dec. 1, 2015
Nicholas Merrill, owner of Calyx Internet Access who received formal demand in 2004 from Federal Bureau of Investigation for information about a customer's account, reveals scope of information that was demanded in national security inquiry.  	           MORE



Nov. 22, 2015
Zeynep Tufekci Op-Ed article argues that intelligence agencies, bearing blame for terrorist attacks like recent assault on Paris, have tendency to automatically call for greater surveillance of technology-based communications and weakening of encryption services; holds efforts are misguided, and that challenge is not collection of more data, but developing ability to identify and track truly dangerous people who currently get lost amid wash of information.	           MORE



Nov. 21, 2015
Board of Airport Commissioners approves plan to create lounge at Los Angeles International Airport allowing celebrities and other high-profile people to enter through private gate and be shuttled to their flights in order to avoid entanglement with paparazzi and delays such activities cause.	           MORE



Nov. 19, 2015
Dr Sandeep Jauhar Op-Ed article opposes appeals court ruling broadening requirement that doctors treating dangerous patients warn potential victims; holds ruling, outcome of lawsuit brought against psychiatrist in Washington State after his patient shot ex-girlfriend, opens door to violations of doctor-patient confidentiality by expanding doctors' obligation to include people who have not specifically been threatened. 	           MORE



Nov. 12, 2015
Microsoft chief executive Satya Nadella announces company will begin using data centers in Germany in order to address concerns raised by European authorities about privacy of Europeans' data in light of revelations about United States government surveillance.	           MORE



Nov. 11, 2015
Facebook plans to appeal Belgian court's privacy ruling that compels company to halt its collection of digital information of people who do not have account with social media service.	           MORE



Nov. 5, 2015
British home secretary Theresa May releases controversial plan to consolidate and clear up piecemeal laws regarding digital data collection and surveillance; human rights advocates see proposal’s requirement that British tech companies store year’s worth of information about websites visited as attack on privacy and Internet security.	           MORE



Nov. 1, 2015
Editorial urges Supreme Court to allow suit filed by Thomas Robins against Spokeo Inc, in which Robins claims company violated Fair Credit Reporting Act, to go forward; disputes claims made by those who support Spokeo that allowing case to go forward would open door to debilitating lawsuits against range of companies.	           MORE



Oct. 28, 2015
Senate passes cybersecurity bill to help companies fight cyberattacks; bill's passage comes after four years of wrangling over privacy protections, making it already largely dated.	           MORE



Oct. 21, 2015
Group of global privacy experts in charge of project called Privacy Bridges will release recommendations for ways European and American regulators can take advantage of European court's striking down of 15-year-old so-called safe harbor agreement; group believes privacy regulators on both sides of Atlantic can improve their cooperation on range of issues.	           MORE



Oct. 12, 2015
Editorial argues that New York State's unique law protecting police officers' disciplinary histories has been stretched too far beyond its original intent, and should be repealed.	           MORE



Oct. 11, 2015
Profile of University of Vienna graduate student Max Schrems, who won landmark privacy case in European Court of Justice that invalidated safe harbor agreement; case, in which he tried to control transfer of his personal data between Europe and United States, has large consequences for firms like Facebook and Google that send data across Atlantic.	           MORE



Oct. 9, 2015
Editorial contends European Court of Justice decision striking down agreement between EU and United States allowing technology companies to store European users' data on American servers is inadequate to protect privacy; holds both United States and Europe must pass stricter privacy laws in order to make real difference; warns European government surveillance programs pose as great threat to privacy as data transfer.	           MORE



Oct. 8, 2015
European Court of Justice strikes down 15-year-old agreement known as safe harbor that permitted transfer of digital data between United States and Europe; ruling raises legal privacy questions for firms like Google and Facebook about moving data.	           MORE



Oct. 7, 2015
European Court of Justice strikes down so-called safe harbor agreement that let companies move information like web search histories and social media use between European Union and United States; rules that agreement gave US government authorities routine access to online information about Europeans, violating European's right to privacy; decision leaves large Internet companies that operate internationally, like Google and Facebook, in kind of legal limbo.	           MORE



Sep. 25, 2015
Dozens of police departments across nation are using complex computer algorithms in attempt to identify people most likely to be involved in future violent crimes, whether as perpetrators or victims; process, known as predictive policing, uses data mining and personal information about individuals, prompting privacy concerns by civil liberties groups.	           MORE



Sep. 25, 2015
United States Postal Service internal investigation finds employees did not safeguard documents that were part of service's law enforcement arm, and include names, addresses and financial information of people suspected of criminal activities; lack of security could compromise privacy of mail and lead to identification of people suspected of criminal activity or people monitored for national security purposes.	           MORE



Sep. 24, 2015
European Court of Justice official says trans-Atlantic 'safe harbor' agreement governing how international firms share customer data does not provide adequate security; opinion could restrict companies like Google and Facebook.	           MORE



Sep. 21, 2015
Retailers and other companies are intensifying their use of consumers' selfies and candid shots on social media sites to promote their brands, creating conflict between marketing objectives and expectations of privacy; consumer advocates and privacy groups have expressed concern.	           MORE



Sep. 10, 2015
Microsoft files request with federal appeals court, based on privacy argument, to block warrant that would allow United States government to force firm to relinquish customer's emails stored on a server located in Ireland.	           MORE



Sep. 8, 2015
Closely watched Justice Department conflicts with Apple and Microsoft over access to their data in crime inquiries highlights how technology companies are increasingly resisting government data demands in effort to show that customer information is protected; standoff has increased between sides as firms begin to use tougher encryption methods.	           MORE



Sep. 6, 2015
Allison Arieff Op-Ed article raises concerns about range of products for so-called Smart Home, saying many of them seem unnecessary; suggests many of these products are only beneficial to companies that produce them, as companies can collect reams of data on consumers.	           MORE



Sep. 3, 2015
Farhad Manjoo State of the Art column contends more than 30 million people who had their accounts hacked on Ashley Madison site deserve sympathy; says many victims, who used site to cheat on their spouses, have been pushed into severe despondency by hacking. 	           MORE



Aug. 31, 2015
More than 180 bills passed by 46 states in last year point to growing concerns about student privacy as classrooms increasingly use learning apps; issues range from possibility of identity theft and predatory marketing to possibility that information about children could be used to negatively affect their employment or college admissions.	           MORE



Aug. 25, 2015
Legal battle surfaces in New Hampshire over whether a display of a marked ballot, like a digital photo taken by a proud voter and shared through social media, is a constitutionally protected form of speech and political expression;  federal court in New Hampshire overturns state's ban on online photos taken in voting booth, but some supporting ban worry that such images threaten privacy and could usher in era of vote-buying and voter intimidation. 	           MORE



Aug. 13, 2015
Police departments around United States are adopting facial recognition software used by miltary in Iraq and Afghanistan in order to track down ordinary criminal suspects; lack of written policies regarding the use of such software, along with little oversight of its use on a regional or national basis, is raising concerns about privacy and misuse.	           MORE



Aug. 13, 2015
Greater New York Hospital Assn requests ban on filming of television reality shows in New York City hospitals without prior written consent from patients; request is prompted by anger over episode of ABC's NY Med, real-life medical series set at New York-Presbyterian Hospital, that showed emergency room death of Manhattan resident Mark Chanko without his family's permission.	           MORE



Aug. 11, 2015
The Upshot; interview with Microsoft Research computer scientist Cynthia Dwork features her discussing how algorithms learn to discriminate, and trade-offs between fairness and privacy.	           MORE



Aug. 11, 2015
Zeynep Tufekci Op-Ed article underscores dangers of so-called Internet of Things, particularly vulnerability of many smart objects connected to digital networks and thus susceptible to hacking; notes also that more pernicious risk such objects pose is violation of consumers' privacy; suggests companies must make initial investment to grow Internet of Things upon more secure foundation, and isolate critical functions on Internet-connected objects. 	           MORE



Aug. 6, 2015
Farhad Manjoo State of the Art column suggests that European law requiring search engines to grant 'right to be forgotten' may spread to United States; observes that rule can  force search engines like Google's to delink results for reasons related to both privacy and accuracy; points out that measures have been strongly opposed by free speech advocates. 	           MORE



Jul. 30, 2015
The search giant said it would not comply with French demands to apply a European data protection ruling to its global domains.	           MORE



Jul. 22, 2015
New York State appeals court rules that Facebook may not challenge search warrants on behalf of its customers; ruling deals blow to civil libertarians and social media companies that had sought to expand user privacy.	           MORE



Jul. 21, 2015
Federal Hipaa law established to guard patients' privacy by protecting their health records is frequently misinterpreted by medical staff and others, and is sometimes used as excuse for people to keep silent on subjects they would rather not talk about; confusion often takes form of overly expansive view of what cannot be disclosed.	           MORE



Jul. 18, 2015
Group of Seattle residents files suit against city, contending practice of checking residents' garbage for improperly sorted recyclables is violation of privacy as protected in Washington State Constitution; at issue is whether items can be considered private after they are thrown away and whether proportions of mis-sorted items can be accurately calculated.	           MORE



Jul. 8, 2015
Report by elite group of security experts contends special access by American and British governments to encrypted communications imperils confidential data and crucial infrastructure; law enforcement and intelligence officials argue encryption impedes efforts to monitor terrorists and other foes.	           MORE



Jun. 29, 2015
Analysis of top 100 United States websites finds companies are increasingly using privacy policies and terms of service that allow transfer of personal data to third parties in the event of merger or other transaction; reflects fact that consumer data may be one of most valuable assets company has; consequences of clauses can be seen in bankruptcy case of True.com, where company tried to sell data on its 43 million members.	           MORE
